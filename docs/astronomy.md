# Astronomy Reference

Scientific and fictional aspects of celestial objects in Helm, with gameplay implications.

## Stars

### Spectral Classification

Stars are classified by surface temperature, which determines their color and properties. The sequence runs from hottest (O) to coolest (M).

| Class | Temperature | Color | Lifespan | Frequency | Examples |
|-------|-------------|-------|----------|-----------|----------|
| O | >30,000K | Blue | 1-10 MY | 0.00003% | Naos, Zeta Puppis |
| B | 10,000-30,000K | Blue-white | 10-100 MY | 0.1% | Rigel, Spica |
| A | 7,500-10,000K | White | 100 MY-1 GY | 0.6% | Sirius, Vega |
| F | 6,000-7,500K | Yellow-white | 1-4 GY | 3% | Procyon, Canopus |
| G | 5,200-6,000K | Yellow | 4-10 GY | 7.5% | Sol, Alpha Centauri A |
| K | 3,700-5,200K | Orange | 10-50 GY | 12% | Epsilon Eridani, Alpha Centauri B |
| M | <3,700K | Red | 50+ GY | 76% | Proxima Centauri, Barnard's Star |

**MY** = million years, **GY** = billion years

#### O-Class (Blue Giants)

**Scientific:** Extremely massive (15-90 solar masses), luminous, and short-lived. Their intense radiation strips away planetary atmospheres and makes habitable zones nearly impossible. Most will end as supernovae.

**In Helm:**
- Rare and dangerous
- Few or no planets (not enough time to form)
- High radiation = ship damage over time
- Exotic materials from stellar wind
- No stations (too hazardous)
- First discovery bonus is high

#### B-Class (Blue-White)

**Scientific:** Hot and luminous, but less extreme than O-class. Still short-lived by stellar standards. Often found in young star clusters.

**In Helm:**
- Uncommon, still hazardous
- May have young, molten worlds
- Good source of exotic gases
- Research stations possible (shielded)
- Valuable for science missions

#### A-Class (White)

**Scientific:** Bright, white stars. Vega and Sirius are A-class. Hot enough to have limited habitable zones but stable enough for planetary systems.

**In Helm:**
- Moderately common
- Can have diverse planetary systems
- Habitable zones possible but narrow
- Often binary systems
- Trading posts occasionally present

#### F-Class (Yellow-White)

**Scientific:** Slightly hotter than the Sun. Good candidates for habitable planets. Stable for billions of years.

**In Helm:**
- Common, welcoming systems
- Wide habitable zones
- Higher chance of ocean/terrestrial worlds
- Stations common
- Good exploration targets

#### G-Class (Yellow - Sun-like)

**Scientific:** Our Sun is G2V. These are the "Goldilocks" stars - stable, long-lived, with well-defined habitable zones. Prime candidates for Earth-like planets.

**In Helm:**
- Most valuable for colonization
- Highest habitable planet probability
- Diverse planetary systems
- Stations very common
- Often contested space

#### K-Class (Orange Dwarfs)

**Scientific:** Cooler and smaller than the Sun, but extremely long-lived (tens of billions of years). Habitable zones are closer in, so planets may be tidally locked.

**In Helm:**
- Very common
- Planets in close orbits
- Tidally locked worlds (one side always facing star)
- Ice mining opportunities (outer planets)
- Mining operations common

#### M-Class (Red Dwarfs)

**Scientific:** The most common stars in the galaxy (76%). Small, cool, and incredibly long-lived. Habitable zone is very close, so planets are likely tidally locked with one side in eternal day, one in eternal night.

**In Helm:**
- Extremely common
- Close-in planetary systems
- Tidally locked worlds with extreme conditions
- Ice and frozen worlds abundant
- Good for long-term mining operations
- Often overlooked = good for hiding

### Stellar Types (Exotic Objects)

Beyond the main sequence, stars can become exotic objects with unique properties.

#### Main Sequence

**Scientific:** Stars in the stable hydrogen-burning phase of their life. Most stars spend 90% of their existence here.

**In Helm:** Normal stars. Standard generation rules apply.

#### Giant / Supergiant

**Scientific:** Stars that have exhausted core hydrogen and expanded dramatically. Red giants can be hundreds of times the Sun's diameter. Eventually shed outer layers or explode.

**In Helm:**
- Expanded habitable zones (temporarily)
- Planets may have been engulfed or pushed outward
- Remnant resources from destroyed inner worlds
- Unstable - flare events possible
- Ancient civilizations might have fled

#### White Dwarf

**Scientific:** The core remnant of a Sun-like star after it sheds its outer layers. Earth-sized but with a solar mass - incredibly dense. No fusion, slowly cooling over billions of years.

**In Helm:**
- Dead systems
- Remnant planets in distant orbits
- Ancient artifacts from pre-collapse civilization
- Exotic dense materials
- Eerie, quiet systems
- Good salvage opportunities

#### Neutron Star

**Scientific:** The collapsed core of a massive star after supernova. City-sized (20km) but 1.4-2 solar masses. Surface gravity is 2 billion times Earth's. Magnetic fields trillions of times stronger than Earth's.

**In Helm:**
- Extremely hazardous
- No planets (destroyed in supernova)
- Exotic materials: neutronium, strange matter
- Gravitational lensing effects
- Navigation hazard
- High-value science targets

#### Pulsar

**Scientific:** A rotating neutron star with beams of radiation shooting from its magnetic poles. Like a lighthouse - we see pulses as the beam sweeps past us. Rotation periods from milliseconds to seconds.

**In Helm:**
- Neutron star + radiation beams
- Timing hazard (beam sweep)
- Useful as navigation beacons (precise timing)
- Extreme danger if caught in beam
- Unique sensor signatures
- No habitation possible

#### Brown Dwarf

**Scientific:** "Failed stars" - too small to sustain hydrogen fusion (13-80 Jupiter masses). Dim, cool, and hard to detect. May have their own planetary systems.

**In Helm:**
- Very dim, easy to miss in surveys
- Often undiscovered = first discovery opportunities
- Can have moons/planets
- Cool enough for close approach
- Good hiding spots
- Unusual chemistry (lithium, methane)

#### Black Hole

**Scientific:** Collapsed core so dense that nothing, not even light, can escape from within the event horizon. Stellar black holes are 3-100 solar masses. Can have accretion disks of infalling matter.

**In Helm:**
- Maximum danger rating
- No direct approach possible
- Accretion disk may have exotic materials
- Time dilation effects near event horizon
- Ultimate mystery/endgame content
- Gravitational lensing distorts star maps

### Multi-Star Systems

#### Binary Stars

**Scientific:** Two stars orbiting their common center of mass (barycenter). Very common - roughly half of all star systems are binaries or higher multiples.

**Orbital configurations:**
- **Wide binary**: Stars far apart, each can have independent planetary systems
- **Close binary**: Stars orbit tightly, planets must orbit both (circumbinary)

**Planet types in binary systems:**
- **S-type (satellite)**: Orbits one star, other star is distant
- **P-type (circumbinary)**: Orbits both stars from outside their orbit

```
Wide Binary (S-type planets possible):
    Star A ←planets    ...gap...    planets→ Star B

Close Binary (P-type planets only):
    [Star A + Star B] ←――― circumbinary planets
```

**In Helm:**
- Binary systems are single "system" entities containing two stars
- Each star has its spectral class and stellar type
- Planets associated with specific star (S-type) or system (P-type)
- Navigation more complex
- Resources potentially doubled
- More interesting exploration

#### Trinary and Higher

**Scientific:** Three or more stars in gravitational relationship. Usually hierarchical - a close pair with a distant third.

**In Helm:**
- Rare
- Complex navigation
- Unstable planetary orbits in some regions
- High discovery value
- Visually spectacular

## Planets

### Planet Classifications

#### Terrestrial

**Scientific:** Rocky planets with solid surfaces, similar to Earth, Mars, or Venus. Composed mainly of silicate rocks and metals. May have atmospheres ranging from none to very dense.

**Size:** 0.5 - 1.5 Earth radii
**Typical orbit:** Inner system (within frost line)

**In Helm:**
- Common around G/K/M stars
- Best candidates for habitability
- Resources: metals, silicates, rare earths
- Surface operations possible
- Can support stations/colonies

#### Super-Earth

**Scientific:** Rocky planets larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune. May have thick atmospheres and intense surface gravity. Some could be "water worlds" with deep global oceans.

**Size:** 1.5 - 2.5 Earth radii
**Mass:** 2 - 10 Earth masses

**In Helm:**
- Higher gravity = more difficult surface ops
- Thicker atmospheres = more resources to extract
- May be habitable despite differences
- Rich in heavy elements
- Valuable mining targets

#### Gas Giant

**Scientific:** Massive planets composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, like Jupiter and Saturn. No solid surface - atmosphere gradually becomes denser until it's liquid/metallic. Have extensive moon systems.

**Size:** 10 - 15 Earth radii
**Mass:** 50 - 500 Earth masses

**In Helm:**
- Cannot land on surface
- Atmospheric mining for hydrogen, helium, deuterium
- Moons are the real targets
- Strong magnetic fields = radiation hazard
- Often have rings
- Moon systems can be mini solar systems

#### Ice Giant

**Scientific:** Like Uranus and Neptune. Smaller than gas giants, with more "ices" (water, ammonia, methane) than hydrogen/helium. Distinctive blue-green colors from methane.

**Size:** 4 - 8 Earth radii
**Mass:** 10 - 50 Earth masses

**In Helm:**
- Atmospheric mining for exotic ices
- Different resource profile than gas giants
- Valuable ammonia and methane
- Extreme cold
- Moons may have subsurface oceans

#### Hot Jupiter

**Scientific:** Gas giants orbiting very close to their stars - orbital periods of days. Tidally locked, with permanent day/night sides. Atmospheres can exceed 1000°C.

**Size:** Jupiter-sized or larger
**Orbit:** < 0.1 AU (extremely close to star)

**In Helm:**
- Exotic atmospheric chemistry
- Extreme heat = unique materials
- Short orbital periods = interesting timing
- No moons (stripped by star's gravity)
- Hazardous but valuable

#### Dwarf Planet

**Scientific:** Small bodies like Pluto, Ceres, Eris. Large enough to be round but haven't cleared their orbital neighborhood. Often found in asteroid belts or outer system.

**Size:** 500 - 2500 km diameter

**In Helm:**
- Easy to miss in surveys
- Low gravity = easy landing/mining
- Often resource-rich (primordial material)
- Good outpost locations
- Many undiscovered = first discovery opportunities

#### Molten

**Scientific:** Planets with surfaces dominated by lava, either from proximity to their star, extreme volcanism, or recent giant impacts. May have magma oceans.

**In Helm:**
- Extremely hazardous surface
- Orbital operations only
- Rich in metals and heavy elements
- Rare materials from volcanic activity
- Time-limited approach windows

#### Frozen

**Scientific:** Worlds beyond the frost line dominated by ices - water, methane, ammonia, nitrogen. Like Pluto or distant moons. May have subsurface liquid water.

**In Helm:**
- Common in outer systems
- Ice mining primary activity
- Subsurface oceans = potential life?
- Low temperatures = easier cooling for equipment
- Good fuel sources (water → hydrogen/oxygen)

#### Ocean

**Scientific:** Planets covered entirely or mostly by liquid water. Could be Earth-like with shallow seas, or deep ocean worlds with hundreds of kilometers of water over a rocky core. Potential for aquatic life.

**Size:** 1 - 2.5 Earth radii

**In Helm:**
- Water is valuable resource
- Potential for life = science value
- Submarine operations?
- Unique extraction methods
- High habitability potential
- Often high priority targets

#### Desert

**Scientific:** Rocky worlds with little to no surface water, like Mars. May have had water in the past. Thin or no atmosphere. Mineral-rich surfaces exposed by lack of weathering.

**In Helm:**
- Easy surface operations (no weather)
- Exposed mineral deposits
- Ancient dried seas = concentrated resources
- Lower habitability
- Good for mining outposts
- Underground water possible

#### Toxic

**Scientific:** Planets with thick, hostile atmospheres like Venus. Extreme surface pressure, corrosive gases (sulfuric acid, chlorine), and runaway greenhouse effects. Surface temperatures can melt lead.

**In Helm:**
- Surface operations nearly impossible
- Atmospheric mining valuable (exotic gases)
- Orbital stations only
- High-altitude floating platforms?
- Unique chemistry
- Danger rating high

#### Ringed

**Scientific:** Planets with visible ring systems, like Saturn. Rings are made of ice and rock particles. Not actually a different planet type - any planet can have rings.

**In Helm:**
- Visual distinction in system map
- Rings are minable (ice, rock)
- Navigation hazard within rings
- Can be combined with any base type
- Spectacular views
- Often indicates moons nearby

## System Features

### Asteroid Belts

**Scientific:** Regions of rocky/metallic debris, usually between inner and outer planets (like our Main Belt) or in the outer system (like the Kuiper Belt).

**In Helm:**
- Primary mining locations
- Resources: iron, nickel, platinum, gold
- Navigation hazard
- Good for hiding
- Can have dwarf planets within

### Debris Fields

**Scientific:** Denser concentrations of material, possibly from planetary collision, destroyed moon, or artificial origin.

**In Helm:**
- Salvage opportunities
- May contain artifacts
- Navigation hazard
- Temporary - may disperse over time
- Story hooks (what happened here?)

### Kuiper Belt Analogue

**Scientific:** Outer system region of icy bodies beyond the gas giants.

**In Helm:**
- Ice mining
- Dwarf planets
- Long-period comets
- Distant, hard to reach
- Undiscovered objects

### Oort Cloud Analogue

**Scientific:** Theoretical shell of icy bodies at the very edge of a star system, loosely bound.

**In Helm:**
- Extreme edge of system
- Comets originate here
- Very sparse
- Interstellar transition zone
- Possible deep space stations
