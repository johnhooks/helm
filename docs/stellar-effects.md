# Stellar Effects

System-wide conditions that modify ship behavior. Every ship in a system is subject to the same effects.

## Overview

A stellar effect is a named modifier that applies to all ships present in a system. Effects modify formula inputs — power regen, sensor range, shield capacity, drive efficiency, scan duration, detection chance. They stack additively. A system's total effect profile is the sum of all active effects from all sources.

Effects aren't buffs you collect. They're environmental conditions you operate within. You don't "have" an ion storm bonus — you're *in* an ion storm, and your ship behaves differently because of it.

## Effect Sources

Effects come from four sources. Each source has different persistence and behavior.

### Stellar (Generated, Constant)

Permanent effects derived from the star and system properties during generation. These never change. They're part of the system's identity.

**Radiation environment.** O and B-type stars produce intense radiation that degrades shields faster but boosts sensor sensitivity (more signal to detect). Red dwarfs are quiet — low radiation, reduced sensor range, but shields last longer.

**Gravitational effects.** Neutron stars and pulsars create intense gravity wells that affect drive efficiency. Jumps into and out of these systems cost more core life. Brown dwarfs have negligible gravity effects — easy to enter and leave.

**Magnetic fields.** Some systems have strong magnetic environments that interfere with specific sensor types. A system with high magnetic activity might degrade the ACU (electromagnetic interference) while boosting the DSC (more contrast in the noise).

**Nebula density.** Systems within or near nebulae have particulate density that reduces scan range but provides natural concealment — harder to find things, but also harder to be found. Good hiding, bad hunting.

These are derived from the existing hazard rating system (see `generation.md`) but broken into specific modifiers rather than a single number. A hazard-8 system near a pulsar has very different effects than a hazard-8 system near an O-type star, even though the overall danger level is similar.

### Random (Generated, Temporary)

Events that occur stochastically and persist for a duration. These are the weather of space.

**Ion storms.** Periodic electromagnetic disturbances that spike sensor noise and drain shield regen. Duration: hours to days. Severity varies. A mild storm is an inconvenience. A severe storm grounds all but the most capable ships.

**Solar flares.** Burst events from the system's star. Brief but intense. Spike radiation, temporarily boost sensor sensitivity (everything lights up), drain capacitor faster. A flare during a scan might reveal something hidden — or might blind the sensor entirely.

**Gravitational waves.** Rare, system-wide perturbations that affect drive calculations. Jump durations become slightly unpredictable. Waypoint positions shift marginally. Minor nuisance for most pilots, serious concern for precision navigation.

**Debris fields.** Temporary hazard zones from collisions, old battles, or natural events. Increase hull damage risk, provide salvage opportunities. Duration: days to weeks, slowly dispersing.

**Resource surges.** Temporary increase in extractable resources from belt instability or geological events. Miners rush in; the system gets busy. Duration: days. Creates natural economic events without developer intervention.

Random effects are generated by the system's seeded RNG on a schedule. They're deterministic — given the seed and the timestamp, the effect is predictable. But players experience them as weather because they don't know the seed.

### Player-Sourced Effects

Effects created by player actions or player-owned infrastructure. These are the most dynamic and politically interesting category.

**Station security field.** A player station with counter-PVP policy projects a security effect across its system. Ships that initiate hostile actions (PNP scanning, interdiction, targeting) within the field trigger a security response. The strength and range of this effect depend on the station's defensive modules. See `stations.md` and `conflict.md`.

**Interdiction field.** An active interdiction creates a localized effect that prevents the target from jumping. The field is detectable by other ships in the system — it's a bright, obvious event.

**Scan emissions.** Active PNP scanning creates a detectable effect. Not a system-wide modifier, but a signal that other ships' passive sensors can pick up. The emission strength scales with scan effort.

**Capital ship presence.** Large ships (future concept) could project system-wide effects simply by being present. A carrier might boost allied sensor range. A dreadnought might project a deterrence field that increases the power cost of hostile actions. A mobile station — effectively a capital ship with docking capability — would project effects similar to a stationary station.

**Mining operations.** Active mining platforms might create localized effects — increased debris, resource depletion signatures, EM noise from extraction equipment. These make mining operations detectable at range.

Player-sourced effects create the political layer. A station's security field defines civilized space. An interdiction field announces conflict. A capital ship's presence reshapes the system's dynamics. The system is what players make of it.

### NPC Effects

Effects generated by NPC systems and game mechanics. These enforce structural rules.

**Hisec security.** Core systems have an NPC security effect that detects and responds to hostile actions. PNP scanning triggers flagging. Interdiction triggers overwhelming response. This isn't a station projecting security — it's a system-level property of core space. See `conflict.md`.

**Other presence.** Systems with active Others (see `others.md`) have effects that reflect alien activity — unusual sensor readings, resource competition, environmental disruption. The effect intensifies as Others establish presence and diminishes as players push them out.

**Trade route activity.** Heavily-traveled systems develop effects from accumulated traffic — more debris, more scan noise, more chances for random encounters. Popular systems are busier and noisier.

**Quarantine.** A future concept: systems flagged by the Origin as quarantined due to extreme hazard, Other infestation, or event conditions. Ships can enter but face severe penalties. A warning, not a wall.

NPC effects provide the structural backbone. Hisec security keeps new players safe. Other presence creates PvE pressure. The Origin can shape the universe through effects without direct mechanical intervention.

## Effect Mechanics

### Stacking

All effects in a system stack additively. If a stellar effect gives -10% sensor range and a nebula gives -15% sensor range, the total is -25% sensor range. If a station projects +20% shield regen and a solar flare gives -30% shield regen, the net is -10%.

No effect categories are special. A player-sourced sensor boost stacks with a stellar sensor penalty the same way two stellar effects would. The math doesn't care about the source.

### Visibility

Effects are visible to ships in the system. The pilot can see what's active:

- Stellar effects are always known (they're constant — learn them once)
- Random effects are detected on entry or when they begin
- Player-sourced effects are detectable with sensors (scan the system to see what's active)
- NPC effects are always known (structural rules)

A pilot jumping into an unfamiliar system sees the stellar effects immediately and can scan for active random and player effects before committing to an action.

### Duration

- **Stellar:** Permanent. Part of the system.
- **Random:** Hours to weeks. Countdown visible to players.
- **Player-sourced:** As long as the source persists. Station security lasts as long as the station is operational. Interdiction lasts as long as the action runs. Scan emissions last as long as the scan runs.
- **NPC:** Persistent until game conditions change.

### Formula Integration

Effects modify formula inputs, not outputs. They change the parameters that feed into the existing formula chain (see `docs/plans/ship-physics.md`), not the results.

For example, an ion storm that reduces shield regen doesn't subtract from the final regen number. It modifies the `regenRate` input before it enters the formula. This means effects interact naturally with everything else — tuning, drift, component stats, envelope phases — without special-case logic.

```
effectiveInput = baseInput × (1 + sum(allEffectModifiers))
```

This keeps the formula chain clean. Effects are just another layer of input modification, evaluated before the formulas run.
